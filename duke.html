Meet Duke Ellington

Duke Ellington (1899, 1974) was a composer who was and is widely considered the best jazz musician of his time. He was born in Washington D.C., where he was exposed to both jazz and ragtime performers. Both influenced his distinctive style. He began to perform and compose professionally at just 17 years old, and soon after became the leader of a 14-man jazz band, which he named the Washingtonians.

His music made a significant break from the musical landscape of the 20s and 30s. While most composers wrote songs and then hired musicians, he tailored his music specifically for his band. In addition, he was a master of setting up soloists. Although the members of his band were excellent musicians in their own right, they were always better musicians with Ellington's music for inspiration.

In his later years, Ellington began to write more thematic music. He wrote songs and scored films about African-American life, including Black, Brown, and Beige, A Rhapsody of Negro Life, and My People
